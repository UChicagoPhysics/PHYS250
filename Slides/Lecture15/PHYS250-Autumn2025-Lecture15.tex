\begin{frame}{Four-Vectors in Particle Physics}

A \textbf{four-vector} is an object that transforms linearly under Lorentz transformations:
\[
x'^{\mu} = \Lambda^{\mu}{}_{\nu}\, x^{\nu}.
\]

\vspace{0.5em}

A typical example is the \textbf{spacetime position four-vector}:
\[
x^{\mu} = \begin{pmatrix}
ct \\
x \\
y \\
z
\end{pmatrix}
\qquad\text{or}\qquad
x_{\mu} = \begin{pmatrix}
ct \\
-\,x \\
-\,y \\
-\,z
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

\vspace{0.5em}

Its \textbf{Lorentz-invariant norm} is:
\[
x^\mu x_\mu = c^2 t^2 - \vec{x}^{\,2}.
\]

\vspace{0.7em}

Other common four-vectors:
\[
p^\mu = \left(E/c,\; \vec{p}\right), \qquad
A^\mu = \left(\phi,\; \vec{A}\right).
\]

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Four-Vectors and LHC Data Analysis}


A \textbf{four-vector} transforms under Lorentz transformations:
\[
p^\mu = (E,\, \vec{p}) .
\]

At the \textbf{LHC}, nearly all reconstructed physics objects  
(jets, muons, electrons, photons) are represented as four-vectors:
\[
p^\mu = (E,\; p_x,\; p_y,\; p_z).
\]

\vspace{0.5em}

Event-level observables are Lorentz-invariant combinations of these:
\[
m^2 = (p_1^\mu + p_2^\mu)(p_{1\mu} + p_{2\mu})
\]
for example, reconstructing the:
\begin{itemize}
    \item invariant mass of the Higgs candidate,
    \item missing transverse momentum for dark matter searches.
\end{itemize}


\end{frame}
